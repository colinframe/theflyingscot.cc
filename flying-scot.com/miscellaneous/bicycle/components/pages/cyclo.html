CYCLO-BENELUX GEARS

"Benelux" was a brand name used by the Cyclo Gear Co.Ltd, of Birmingham, England. Introduced in 1949 along with the first of the Cyclo "Benelux" rear derailleurs, it was initially applied to those products aimed at the more 'sporting' end of the market.

"Benelux" (Original 1949 version)

Benelux "Mark 7"

Available in four types ;

3-speed (1/8" chain)
4-speed (1/8" chain)
4-speed (3/32" chain)
5-speed (3/32" chain)

Recommended freewheel sizes

1/2 x 1/8 inch chain - 3 & 4-speed 14-20
1/2 x 3/32 inch chain - 4-speed 14-20, 5-speed 14-22

Range = 14-24 teeth on freewheel, 4-tooth difference on chainwheel & 14-tooth overall.

Improvements shown were ; toggle chain replacement costs reduced, easily adjustable to different ratios, easier lateral adjustment, improved design of chainguard, flush cage, and 10% lighter.

Benelux "Super 60"

First Cyclo design of 'pantograph' derailleur, and with pre-set chain tension.

Available in two types ;

4-speed (3/32" chain)
5-speed (3/32" chain)

Recommended freewheel sizes

1/2 x 3/32 inch chain - 4-speed 14-23, 5-speed 14-25

Range = 13-26 teeth on freewheel, 4-tooth difference on chainwheel & 17-tooth overall.

Benelux "Sport P2"

Although both the French and British Cyclo companies continued for a long number of years to use the corporate 'Cyclo' logo, they were no longer part of the same concern.

Sadly in November 1999, Cyclo succumbed to voluntary liquidation with the loss of 30 jobs. The name lives on however, having been bought by the British tool and accessories company Weldtite the following month.




There were a whole sequence of Cyclo Benelux racing derailleurs and a few touring ones too. And several varieties of front derailleur. Initially they launched one racing rear derailleur with a fairly straight aluminium arm - this what I have on sale on Ebay right now. It was replaced less than a year later by one with a cranked aluminium arm. In 1953 they replaced this with a cranked steel arm version - one of which I will be offering to CR members this afternoon. Slightly later (1955ish from memory) this was updated by the Mark VII and there was a Mark 12 which was adjustable for different freewheels.
